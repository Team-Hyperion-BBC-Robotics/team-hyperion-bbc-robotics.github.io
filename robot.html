CAD Design

STRUCTURAL & DESIGN

Overview

For Structural and Electrical we use the CAD program Autodesk Fusion 360 to 3D model and design our PCB’s. The machinery used to make and manufacture our design was the Bambu Lab 3D printer with PLA and ABS filament, as well as a CNC miller with 3mm thick aluminum. We order PCBs from the company JLCPCB. The components of these boards are ordered from a variety of websites and companies, but we try and order them from Digikey, making them our main website for ordering components.

Chassis

The two main plates of the robot are CNC-machined from 3 mm 6061 aluminium. This design helps keep the centre of mass low to the ground, improving stability during rapid movement, while also providing increased strength and durability during impacts.

Movement System

The drive system consists of four motors arranged in a diagonal cross (X-shape), providing improved power, speed, and balance for full 360-degree movement of the robot. The motors are secured using custom motor brackets to ensure proper alignment and structural stability.

To enable this omnidirectional movement, the motors are paired with double omnidirectional wheels. These allow the robot to move smoothly in any direction while maintaining good grip due to increased surface contact with the field. The wheel frames are made from ABS filament, while the rollers are made from silicone with a metal core. The rollers are mounted around a metal axle to keep them securely in place.

DCX19 16:1 9V Maxon motors were selected for their compact size and relatively high torque. They are powered at 12 V to increase their overall power output.

Vision / Detection Systems

For ball detection, 24 infrared sensors are soldered onto the main board and arranged in a circular pattern. A custom 3D-printed mount separates the sensors, allowing for accurate and precise detection of the ball’s direction and signal strength.

For line detection, a custom-made PCB is mounted to the underside of the robot. It features two concentric rings, enabling fast and reliable detection of the white boundary line.

The vision system uses an OpenMV camera pointed upwards towards a cone-shaped reflective foil surface. This reflector is supported by a clear acrylic tube, allowing for full 360-degree vision of the field and clear detection of the coloured goals. The acrylic tube is mounted on an adjustable plate, enabling calibration of the mirror’s position to account for small inconsistencies in the camera setup.

Electrical · PCB Design and Power

ELECTRONICS & SENSORS

Main Board

The main board consists of a power regulation circuit, ball detection sensors, a camera, a Bluetooth module, a compass, dual microcontrollers, and motor driver circuits. The robot is powered by a 12.6 V, 1300 mAh LiPo battery, which is connected to the main board via an XT-60 extension cable.

The 12 V supply is connected to five motor controllers (VNH7070). Four of these control the drive motors, while the fifth controls the dribbler motor. The 12 V supply, along with 3.3 V and ground, is also connected via a three-pin JST connector to a custom kicker control circuit.

The 12 V supply is stepped down to 5 V and then to 3.3 V. The 5 V rail powers the dual microcontrollers (Teensy 4.1), camera (OpenMV H7), Bluetooth module (HC-05), and photogate. The 3.3 V rail supplies the 24 infrared sensors (TSSP77038TR), the compass (BNO055), and a custom light ring PCB through a 12-pin FFC cable.

Light Ring Board

The Light Sensor Board consists of 48 groups of components, each including phototransistors, LEDs, resistors, and smoothing capacitors. The first 16 groups are arranged into four segments, with each segment containing four groups positioned near the outer edges of the robot between the wheels. This placement allows the robot to detect and respond to the line more quickly.

The remaining 32 groups are arranged in a circular pattern in front of the wheels, enabling the defender to position itself closer to the goal without entering the goal box. Each group produces a signal that is routed through three 16-bit multiplexers, which output four analog pins. Therefore, reducing the number of pins required on the main processor.

Software · Orientation & Stability

BNO055 IMU System

An Overview

In the heat of a match, the robot is constantly bumped, spun, and pushed by other players. Without a sense of direction, it would quickly become disoriented, driving toward its own goal by mistake. The BNO055 Inertial Measurement Unit (IMU) acts as the robot's "Inner Ear," providing a rock-solid sense of balance and heading.

Maintaining North

While the TSSP "eyes" find the ball, the IMU keeps the robot facing the correct way. We use the IMUPLUS mode, which combines accelerometer and gyroscope data to give us a stable "Heading" (the 0 to 360-degree angle the robot is facing).

  • Compass Reset: When we flick the calibration switch, the code resets the sensor's orientation. This defines "Front" for the rest of the game.
  • Drift Compensation: Standard gyroscopes "drift" over time, but the BNO055 uses internal algorithms to keep the heading precise even during high-speed collisions.
  • Active Correction: No matter which way the robot is told to drive, it is always trying to fight the rotation of the wheels to stay facing the opponent's goal.

The Stability Math

To keep the robot straight, we use a PID Controller (Proportional, Integral, Derivative). The IMU tells us our current angle, and we compare it to our target (usually 0°). The difference between them is the "Error." The robot then applies a counter-rotation (Correction) to cancel out that error.

// 1. Get the current bearing from the IMU bno.getEvent(&bearing); float currentHeading = bearing.orientation.x; // 2. Normalise the angle to [-180, 180] for easier math float error = com.normaliseAngle180(currentHeading); // 3. Calculate Correction (_cor) using PID _cor = -correction.update(error, 0.0); // 4. Apply to Motors motors.run(_spd, _dir, _cor);

Key Functions

begin()Initializes the sensor via I2C and checks if the "Inner Ear" is actually plugged in.
setExtCrystalUse()Tells the sensor to use an external clock for higher mathematical precision.
getEvent()Snaps a picture of the robot's current orientation in 3D space.
normaliseAngle180()A helper function that ensures the robot understands that 359° is actually just 1° away from 0°.

Software · Ball Detection

TSSP System

An Overview

Imagine our robot is standing in the middle of a dark room. Somewhere in that room, there is a glowing orange ball. Our goal is to find that ball and kick it into a goal. We use two electronic "brains" (called Teensys) to do this. One brain acts like the Eyes, and the other acts like the Captain.

The "Secondary" Teensy

This part of the code is responsible for looking around. We have several sensors (TSSP sensors) placed in a circle around the robot. These sensors are like little eyes that can only see infrared light from the ball.

  • The infrared ball blinks very fast. The "Eyes" look at each sensor 255 times in a row. Every time a sensor sees a flash of light, it adds a point.
  • If the sensor on the front-right has 200 points and the one on the back has 0, the robot knows the ball is somewhere to the front-right.
  • The code takes the top four strongest sensor readings and combines them using vector summation to calculate both the angle and strength of the ball signal.

The "Eyes" Math

The system identifies the ball's position by isolating the four highest sensor readings. Each sensor is treated as a vector, where the physical orientation of the sensor represents the angle and the point count represents the magnitude. By plotting these on a Cartesian plane and performing a vector summation, the robot calculates a single resultant vector.

// TSSP Update — Ball Direction and Strength Calculation for (i = 0; i < 255; i++) { tsspValues[j] += 1 - digitalRead(tsspPins[j]); } x = sum(tsspSortedValues * cos(sensorAngle)); y = sum(tsspSortedValues * sin(sensorAngle)); ballStr = ((3 * top[0]) + (2 * top[1]) + top[2] + top[3]) / 7.0; if (ballStr != 0) { ballDir = 360 - fmod((RAD_TO_DEG * atan2f(y, x)) - 90, 360); } else { ballDir = 0; }

The "Captain" (Primary's) Math

The Captain's brain uses exponential functions to make decisions. Instead of a simple turn, it calculates a Strength Factor based on the ball's signal intensity. This factor determines the Angle Shift — the mathematical curve required to orbit the ball.

// 1. Normalize ball direction to [-180, 180] float relativeDir = (bInfo.dir() > 180) ? (bInfo.dir() - 360) : bInfo.dir(); // 2. Calculate Strength Factor float ratio = constrain(bInfo.str() / ORBIT_TUNER, 0.0, 1.0); float strengthFactor = constrain(0.02 * ratio * expf(4.5 * ratio), 0.0, 1.0); // 3. Calculate Angle Shift (Orbit Offset) float angleEffect = min(0.4 * expf(0.25 * abs(relativeDir)) - 0.4, 90.0); float totalOffset = strengthFactor * angleEffect; // 4. Apply direction adjustment and scale speed float adjustment = (relativeDir < 0) ? -totalOffset : totalOffset; mInfo.dir = fmod(bInfo.dir() + adjustment, 360.0); mInfo.spd = BASE_SPEED + (SURGE_SPEED - BASE_SPEED) * (1.0 - totalOffset / 90.0);

Software · Line Avoidance

LIGHT SYSTEM

The LightSystem library detects the white boundary lines of the RoboCup field via an underside IR phototransistor array. Each sensor emits IR light downward and measures the reflected intensity — the white field lines reflect significantly more than the dark playing surface, producing a clear contrast spike that the library converts into a line direction and state value. In the state selection priority chain, STATE_LINE_AVOID sits immediately after calibration — meaning it pre-empts both attack and defend behaviour the instant any line is detected.

Avoidance Logic

When ls.get_line_dir() returns anything other than -1, the robot is on the line. The avoidance direction is computed as (line_dir + 180°) mod 360 — the exact opposite of where the line was detected.

Vertical Correction in Defend

The light system also plays a role in the defender's vector sub-state outside of full line avoidance mode. The get_line_state() value feeds a vertical PID (vert) that gently pushes the defender away from the back wall.

Hardware — Multiplexed Sensor Ring

Rather than dedicating one microcontroller pin per sensor, the system uses a 4-bit binary multiplexer scheme. Four digital output pins (inPin[0–3]) select which of up to 32 sensors to read, addressed via two shared analog pins (LIGHT_PIN / LIGHT_PIN2). Sensors 0–15 are read from one channel; 16–31 from the other. This allows the full sensor ring to be polled using only 6 GPIO pins total.

// inner_read() — Binary multiplexer selection for (uint8_t i = 0; i < 4; i++) { digitalWrite(inPin[i], (sNum >> i) & 1); } return (sNum >= 16) ? analogRead(LIGHT_PIN) : analogRead(LIGHT_PIN2);

Direction Algorithm — Cluster Detection

The core of calculate_line_dir() works in three stages. First, sensors above the white threshold (LS_THRESH) that are contiguous with their neighbours are grouped into clusters — arcs of sensors simultaneously seeing the line. Up to 4 clusters are tracked. Each cluster's angular midpoint is then computed by averaging start and end indices and scaling to degrees. Finally, the number of clusters determines how the final line direction is resolved:

①
1 Cluster

Line direction = midpoint of the single detected cluster.

②
2 Clusters

Average angle between both midpoints, with 360° wraparound compensation applied.

③
3 Clusters

Outermost two clusters are averaged — the central cluster is discarded.

⊘
0 Clusters

No line detected. lineDirection is set to -1 and the state machine takes over.

A special wraparound correction handles the edge case where a cluster spans the boundary between the last and first sensor in the ring — ensuring the midpoint is computed correctly across the 0/NUM_LS seam.

State Machine — calculate_line_state()

A four-state machine tracks the robot's relationship to the line across frames. Transitions are driven by whether lineDirection != -1 (line detected) and whether the measured direction has shifted 140° or more between cycles — indicating the robot has crossed fully over the line rather than tracked along it.

State 0 — OffNo line detected. Enters State 1 the moment any sensor cluster appears.
State 1 — OnRobot is tracking the line. Transitions to State 2 if direction flips ≥ 140°.
State 2 — CrossingRobot has crossed over the line. Transitions to State 3 if line contact is lost.
State 3 — ExitedLine was crossed and then lost. Previous direction is restored via dead-reckoning.

In State 3, the update() loop restores prevLineDirection rather than using the current (lost) value. This keeps the avoidance vector valid for the brief moment the robot is airborne over the boundary — preventing erratic direction searching when contact is momentarily broken.

Avoidance Logic

When ls.get_line_dir() returns anything other than -1, the robot is on the line. The avoidance direction is simply the exact opposite — computed as (line_dir + 180°) mod 360. Speed is scaled by a PID controller fed from get_line_state(), so the robot brakes harder the deeper into the line it has gone.

// STATE_LINE_AVOID — Full avoidance response _spd = -lineAvoid.update(ls.get_line_state(), 0.0); _dir = fmod(ls.get_line_dir() + 180.0, 360); // LWDEFEND_VECTOR — Soft wall pushback for Defender float vertVect = vert.update(ls.get_line_state(), 1.6);

Vertical Correction in Defend

Outside of full line avoidance mode, the light system feeds a secondary role in the defender's LWDEFEND_VECTOR sub-state. The raw get_line_state() value drives a vertical PID (vert) that gently pushes the defender away from the back wall — a soft, continuous correction that keeps the robot out of the danger zone without triggering a hard avoidance manoeuvre.

Software · Vision

OPENMV CAMERA

The camera script runs as a standalone MicroPython program on the OpenMV RT — a dedicated vision co-processor that handles all colour detection entirely independently of the main Teensy. Every frame, it hunts for three coloured objects on the field, finds their pixel centroids, and fires the results over UART to the Teensy in a compact byte packet. The Teensy never touches raw image data — it only ever sees the final coordinates.

Camera Initialisation — Locked Exposure

Before the main loop begins, the camera is configured to produce deterministic, reproducible images regardless of the ambient lighting in the competition venue. Auto-gain, auto-white-balance, and auto-exposure are all explicitly disabled. Exposure is fixed at 12,000 µs and the frame is cropped to a 120×120 pixel window centred on the sensor — reducing processing time and focusing the blob detector on the area directly in front of the robot.

# Locked exposure — no automatic adjustments sensor.set_auto_gain(False) sensor.set_auto_whitebal(False) sensor.set_auto_exposure(False, exposure_us=12000) sensor.set_windowing((120, 120)) # crop to centre window

Locking the exposure is critical at competition. RoboCup venues vary wildly in lighting — a camera left on auto would constantly shift its colour interpretation between matches, causing blobs to disappear or false-positive on the wrong target. With a fixed exposure the team can tune thresholds once in the pits and trust them to hold for the entire match.

Colour Thresholds — LAB Colour Space

Detection uses the LAB colour space rather than RGB. LAB separates luminance from colour information, making the detector far more robust to changes in brightness. Each threshold tuple defines a bounding box in LAB space: (L_min, L_max, A_min, A_max, B_min, B_max).

Index 1 — Yellow GoalHigh L, positive A, high positive B — bright warm yellow. Targets the team's attack goal.
Index 2 — Blue GoalHigh L, negative B — cool blue. Targets the opponent's defend goal.
Index 4 — Orange BallThe IR-reflective orange ball sits in a distinct LAB region separate from both goals.

Note that blob.code() returns a bitmask corresponding to which threshold index matched — 1, 2, or 4 — not a zero-based index. The data array uses these codes directly as slot indices to keep assignment unambiguous.

Blob Detection & Centroid Extraction

img.find_blobs() scans the cropped frame for connected regions matching any of the three thresholds simultaneously. Small noise regions are filtered out via pixels_threshold=10 and area_threshold=10. Overlapping blobs of the same colour are merged into a single region. The result list is then sorted by area descending — so if multiple blobs of the same colour appear (reflections, partial occlusion), only the largest is used.

# Find, merge, and sort all colour blobs by area blobs = img.find_blobs(t, pixels_threshold=10, area_threshold=10, merge=True) blobs = sorted(blobs, key=lambda blob: -blob.area()) # Write centroid (cx, cy) into the correct slot by blob colour code data = [[-1,-1],[-1,-1],[-1,-1]] for blob in blobs: data[blob.code()] = [blob.cx(), blob.cy()]

Each slot in data initialises to [-1, -1]. If a colour is not detected in a given frame, its slot remains -1 — a sentinel value the Teensy uses to determine that the target is not currently visible.

UART Packet — Byte Stream to Teensy

Once centroids are resolved, a compact 8-byte packet is sent over UART at 115,200 baud. The packet opens with two fixed header bytes (200, 122) that the Teensy uses to frame-sync and validate incoming data, followed by the X and Y centroid of each of the three colour slots in order.

# Packet structure: [200, 122, cx1, cy1, cx2, cy2, cx3, cy3] uart.writechar(200) # Header byte 1 uart.writechar(122) # Header byte 2 for i in range(3): uart.writechar(data[i][0]) # cx — horizontal centroid (-1 if unseen) uart.writechar(data[i][1]) # cy — vertical centroid (-1 if unseen)
Why pixel coordinates, not angles? Sending raw cx/cy values keeps the OpenMV's job minimal and fast — it does no trigonometry. The Teensy's Camera library converts pixel position to field angle using the known camera FOV, and handles the -1 sentinel to gate goal-tracking and kicker logic accordingly.

Software · Teensy-Side Processing

CAMERA C++ LIBRARY

Once the OpenMV fires its 8-byte packet over UART, all the heavy lifting shifts to the Teensy's Camera C++ library. This layer is responsible for validating the incoming stream, converting raw pixel centroids into real-world centimetres, computing angles and distances to each target, and finally producing the movement vectors the strategy loop actually drives from.

Initialisation — Camera::init()

A single call to init() opens the hardware serial port connected to the OpenMV at 115,200 baud — matching the rate the MicroPython script writes at. This must be called once during setup before the main loop ever calls update().

void Camera::init() { cameraSerial.begin(115200); }

Packet Reception & Validation — Camera::update()

Every call to update() attempts to consume exactly one vision packet from the serial buffer. The function first checks that enough bytes are available, then reads the first byte and peeks at the second — if the pair does not match the expected header (200, 122), the call returns immediately without touching any goal or ball state. This prevents stale or partially-received packets from corrupting the robot's world model.

void Camera::update(bool attackBlue) { if (cameraSerial.available() < CAM_PACKET_SIZE) return; int b1 = cameraSerial.read(); int b2 = cameraSerial.peek(); if (b1 != CAM_START_PACK_1 || b2 != CAM_START_PACK_2) return; cameraSerial.read(); // consume the peeked header byte // ... read goalXY, goalYY, goalXB, goalYB, ballX, ballY }

Coordinate Shift — Camera::shift()

Raw pixel values from the OpenMV are centred on the top-left corner of its 120×120 crop window. shift() re-origins both axes to the centre of the robot by subtracting a fixed CAMERA_PIXEL_SHIFT offset. This is skipped entirely when a coordinate is 0 — the sentinel for "object not seen" — so invisible targets are never accidentally re-mapped.

void Camera::shift(int& x, int& y) { if (x != 0) { x -= CAMERA_PIXEL_SHIFT; y -= CAMERA_PIXEL_SHIFT; } }

Pixel → Centimetre Conversion — Camera::px_to_cm()

A linear regression formula maps the pixel scale to real-world distance. The coefficients were derived empirically by placing known objects at measured distances in front of the camera and fitting a line through the resulting pixel readings. This gives the Teensy an approximate physical scale it can use for distance and angle maths.

float Camera::px_to_cm(int px) { return (0.361429 * px) - 36.18095; }
Why linear regression? The perspective projection through a wide-angle lens is non-linear, but over the relatively small depth range the robot encounters on a RoboCup field the linear approximation is accurate enough to guide goal-tracking and kicker decisions without the overhead of a full perspective model.

Distance & Angle Calculation

Two helpers turn the centimetre-space (x, y) displacement into the polar form the strategy layer actually uses. calc_distance() applies the standard Euclidean norm, while calc_angle() uses atan2f and then rotates the result by 90° to align zero-degrees with the robot's forward axis rather than the mathematical convention of pointing along the positive x-axis.

float Camera::calc_distance(float x, float y) { return sqrtf(pow(x, 2) + pow(y, 2)); } float Camera::calc_angle(float opp, float adj) { // Rotate 90° so 0° = straight ahead, not mathematical east return fmod(90 - (atan2f(opp, adj) * RAD_TO_DEG), 360); }

Goal Assignment — Attack vs. Defend

The update() call receives a single attackBlue flag from the strategy layer. Depending on this flag, the yellow and blue goal pixel streams are swapped between the attackGoal and defendGoal objects — meaning the rest of the codebase never needs to know which physical colour it is tracking, only whether it is attacking or defending. Visibility is gated on the Y coordinate being non-zero (the -1 / 0 sentinel from the OpenMV).

if (attackBlue) { attackGoal._angle = calc_angle(cmgoalYB, cmgoalXB); attackGoal._dist = calc_distance(cmgoalXB, cmgoalYB); attackGoal._visible = (goalYB != 0); defendGoal._angle = calc_angle(cmgoalYY, cmgoalXY); defendGoal._dist = calc_distance(cmgoalXY, cmgoalYY); defendGoal._visible = (goalYY != 0); } else { // Swap: yellow becomes attack, blue becomes defend attackGoal._angle = calc_angle(cmgoalYY, cmgoalXY); // ... mirror assignments }

Ball Orbit Vector — Open Mode

When the robot is in open / attacker mode (OPEN == true), update() computes a curved approach vector for the ball rather than a straight-line heading. The same exponential orbit algorithm used by the TSSP primary is re-applied here using camera-derived ball position — so the robot arcs around to get behind the ball before committing to a shot, rather than driving straight at it and deflecting it sideways.

float relativeDir = (ballInfo._angle > 180) ? (ballInfo._angle - 360) : ballInfo._angle; float ratio = constrain(ballInfo._dist / ORBIT_TUNER, 0.0f, 1.0f); float strengthFactor = constrain(0.02f * ratio * expf(4.5f * ratio), 0.0f, 1.0f); float angleEffect = min(0.4f * expf(0.25f * abs(relativeDir)) - 0.4f, 90.0f); float totalOffset = strengthFactor * angleEffect; float directionAdjustment = (relativeDir < 0) ? -totalOffset : totalOffset; float speedReduction = totalOffset / 90.0f; ballInfo._moveAngle = fmod(ballInfo._angle + directionAdjustment, 360.0f); ballInfo._moveSpeed = BASE_SPEED + (SURGE_SPEED - BASE_SPEED) * (1.0f - speedReduction);

Localisation — Camera::localise_to()

The camera can also reconstruct the robot's absolute field position. If either goal is visible, the known goal positions on the field (±85 cm from centre) are combined with the measured goal angle and distance to back-calculate the robot's (x, y) coordinates. localise_to() then computes the magnitude and heading needed to reach any arbitrary target coordinate — used by the defender to hold a position on the field rather than just react to the ball.

// Reconstruct robot position from defend goal robotData._x = 0 - (defendGoal.dist() * cos(defendGoal.angle() * DEG_TO_RAD)); robotData._y = -CENTER_TO_GOAL - (defendGoal.dist() * sin(defendGoal.angle() * DEG_TO_RAD)); // Then navigate toward a target coordinate robotData._moveMag = sqrt(pow(robotData._x - targX, 2) + pow(robotData._y - targY, 2)); robotData._moveAngle = atan2f(targY - robotData._y, targX - robotData._x) * RAD_TO_DEG;
Fallback hierarchy: localise_to() prefers the defend goal for position estimation since it is always behind the robot and provides the most stable reference. If only the attack goal is visible it falls back to that. If neither goal is seen, movement magnitude drops to zero and the robot holds still — preventing blind charging when the vision pipeline momentarily loses both targets.

Software · Inter-Robot Communication

BLUETOOTH COMMUNICATION

An Overview

Imagine we have two robots on a soccer field. If both robots chase the ball at the same time, they will crash into each other or leave the goal wide open. To play as a team, they need to talk. We use HC-05 Bluetooth modules to let the robots "whisper" to each other across the field.

How they Talk (The "Packet" System)

The robots don't just send random numbers; they send a structured "envelope" of information called a Packet. Every message starts with two Start Bytes (the number 255 twice) to ensure valid data. The packet contains: Ball Strength, Ball Direction, Goal Angle, Goal Distance, Battery Level, and X/Y coordinates.

How they Decide (The "Role Weighting" Logic)

Each robot calculates a "Score" for itself and its teammate based on several factors. The robot with the higher score becomes the Attacker. Factors include Ball Strength (0-40pts), Attack Cone (0 or 20pts), Goal Alignment (0 or 15pts), Defensive Gap (0-15pts), and Battery Level (0-10pts).

Fail-Safe's

In the heat of a game, things go wrong. Dead Radio: if Bluetooth disconnects, the robot immediately defaults to Defender. Blind spots: if neither robot can see the ball, they stop switching roles. Battery/Fuel Rule: if a robot's battery is critically low, it is forced into Defender role.

int Bluetooth::roleWeighting(const SendData& r) { // 1. Give points for being close to the ball int score = r.ballStr * 0.4f; // 2. Bonus if ball is right in front score += (r.ballDir <= 30 || r.ballDir >= 330) ? 20 : 0; // 3. Bonus if we are facing the goal we want to hit score += (r.goalAng <= 25 || r.goalAng >= 335) ? 15 : 0; return score; }
